I am an applied mathematician who is broadly compelled by explaining the cell with pure and applied math. Currently, I am a Systems Biology PhD candidate at Harvard University advised by Dr. Jeremy Gunawardena. My doctoral research involves using graph theory and stochastic processes to understand how cellular information processing tasks, such as those in eukaryotic gene regulation, depart from thermodynamic equilibrium. I was a recipient of a scholarship funded by the NSF-Simons Center for Mathematical and Statistical Analysis of Biology at Harvard. Through a collaboration with Dr. Polly Yu, I contributed to a publication on disguised toric dynamical systems.

As an undergraduate, I spent two summers as a research assistant with Dr. Alex Chavez in George Church’s lab, where I worked on projects developing CRISPR/Cas9 transcriptional activators (which resulted in a Nature Methods publication) and neurodegenerative screens. I graduated from Middlebury College in 2018 with a B.A. in Mathematics and Biochemistry. My mathematics senior thesis in fluid dynamics focused on characterizing stability in laminar flows and the hydrodynamics of magnetotactic bacteria.
