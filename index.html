I am a mathematician interested in exploring biochemical reaction networks and the geometry and topology of multi-scale biological systems. As of July 2024, I am a Van Loo Independent Postdoctoral Fellowship at the University of Michigan Ann Arbor, co-hosted by the Michigan Center for Applied and Interdisciplinary Mathematics and Center for the Study of Complex Systems. I completed my PhD in Systems Biology PhD at Harvard University advised by Jeremy Gunawardena. My doctoral work explores graph-theoretic models of biochemical reaction networks, primarily through the linear framework. My background in graph theory and stochastic processes allows me to investigate open problems across disciplines, ranging from non-equilibrium thermodynamics to molecular information processing. I also have done work in chemical reaction network theory with Polly Yu on disguised toric systems, which is published in SIAM Journal of Dynamical Systems.

My goal is to become a professor in applied mathematics, and the break down the barriers between pure and applied math in pursuit of uncovering compelling descriptions of biological systems. While at Harvard, I was a recipient of multiple scholarships funded by the NSF-Simons Center for Mathematical and Statistical Analysis of Biology at Harvard and the Lynch Foundation. My mathematics senior thesis in fluid dynamics focused on characterizing stability in laminar flows and the hydrodynamics of magnetotactic bacteria.
